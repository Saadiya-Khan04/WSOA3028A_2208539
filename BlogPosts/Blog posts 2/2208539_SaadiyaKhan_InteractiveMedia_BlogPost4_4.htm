Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics - Fernanda B. Viégas and Martin Wattenberg

 

Wattenberg and Viégas discuss and research information visualization in an artistic context, this describes how artists use scientific techniques in order to create a form of data visualization using art.

“Data visualization is usually viewed as a tool to support analytic reasoning.” (Wattenberg, 2007, p. 182). The authors describe data visualization as a way of explaining and describing why and what the data is about in a specific article. The authors discuss that in order for it to be classified as artistic data visualization, the art should be based on real data. The authors mention that artistic data visualization is not developed to be aesthetically pleasing but rather why it is developed and how it functions.

Wattenberg and Viégas research the qualities that artistic data visualizations have by looking at examples from existing forms of artistic data visualization. They mention that information visualization can help audiences to see data patterns such as trends, maximum and minimum data value sets. There are different examples that are explored, through specifically Jason Salavon’s work, showing the similarities, differences and changes over a few pieces of art throughout the decades, such as houses for sale, class yearbook pictures and even narrative changes.

The idea of these art pieces are to take different pictures of a similar category and manipulate the picture by making the pixels bigger and blurring them out. These artists use this technique, while still keeping the original colour scheme, which then allows viewers to see the similarities and differences in these images. Another artist that is discussed in this paper and uses the same technique as Jason Salavon is Golan Levin.

Golan Levin makes use of a similar method, but uses it to manipulate data sets with numbers and letters, this has its own set of rules on how to interpret, read and understand this specific data visualization technique. 

Jussi Ängeslevä and Ross Cooper had also used art to visualize a data set, but instead of using images or numbers, they used video footage to record time. Instead of showing the changes in things over time and using a number data set, this shows the changes of places or settings over time.

The authors mention how social networking has also become important and useful when it comes to analysing data. This makes use of objects and places in communities that are represented and people can make use of these data sets.

In conclusion, these forms of artistic data visualization can be helpful and expressive, although some are just purely aesthetic. The clock by Jussi Ängeslevä and Ross Cooper is purely aesthetic because the images cannot be helpful for real-time surveillance, although Jason Salavon’s image distortions can be helpful by seeing average trends over time, through images.

References

Wattenberg, F. B. V. a. M., 2007. Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics. Schuler D. (eds) Online Communities and Social Computing, Volume 4564, pp. 182 - 191.
